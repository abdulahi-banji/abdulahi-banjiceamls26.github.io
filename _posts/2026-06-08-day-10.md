what_i_learned: |
  The day started with our breakout room session. My team and I went over our project plan, and then spent the rest of the time reading literature before giving an overview of what we all learned. One of the papers I read was on YOLO-WAS, a lightweight apple detection model built on top of YOLO11. Reading through it gave me a better understanding of how object detection models are structured, specifically how the backbone, neck, and head work together. I also got familiar with terms like mAP, precision, recall, GFLOPs, and IoU thresholds, which are key metrics used to evaluate how well a detection model performs. On the data side, the researchers collected over 1600 images of three apple varieties under different lighting conditions and distances, then used augmentation techniques like flipping, scaling, and noise addition to expand the dataset to over 4000 images. That part was interesting because it showed how much work goes into building a quality dataset before any training even happens. The model ended up hitting a precision of 0.958 and an mAP@50 of 0.970 while cutting parameter count by over 22 percent compared to the baseline, which showed that you can make a model lighter without sacrificing accuracy. We also spent time discussing LabelMe and the annotation patterns we noticed across the papers, then wrapped up by planning out what tomorrow looks like as we get ready to install and work with LabelMe hands-on.
