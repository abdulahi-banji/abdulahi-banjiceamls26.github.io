what_i_learned: |
  The day started with me excitedly reviewing the results from our model training yesterday. We had used 200 epochs and changed a few values in our data augmentation settings, and the model produced a result at least twice as accurate as the one from before. After that, we watched videos together as a team to solidify our knowledge of ML training and learnt terminologies anbd concepts that are often used to evaluate model results. We learned about IoU (intersection over union), a couple of formulas to calculate it, IoU thresholds, confusion matrices, and true and false positives and negatives. It was an educational watch.

  Our faculty mentor then came by with our camera and the Lego pieces. We had to write code with one function to detect a Lego piece and another to classify the object detected. I studied the code for a bit, and it wasn't complex. We mounted the camera, connected it to our device, and watched it detect the Lego bricks and classify them based on the classes it had been trained on. I noticed it also picked up the glasses sitting on the table, which means the model will need more training and augmentation. Another major improvement we still need to make is getting it to run in real time. It was exciting to see our project come to life and to see real progress toward our goal. After that, we went over the literature for our paper, where I was responsible for writing the methodology and technical sections. I made solid progress filling in my part.

  My faculty mentor then suggested we use an actual Python file instead of a Jupyter notebook to train the model, so we could train everything in one run. Our objective was to train across YOLOv8, YOLOv8-p2, YOLOv11, and YOLO26, each in the nano, small, medium, large, and x variants, to find the most accurate result. From there, we planned to take the best-performing model and refine it further by adjusting augmentation values and increasing the number of epochs. I started writing the code manually for a system that would train each model in sequence and exit once every model had finished. Once we understood the objective and had a draft, I had Claude help me complete and revise the code. It ended up being almost 100 lines, but it was straightforward. we created a new folder, ran the code there, and left it training overnight so we could come back tomorrow to analyze the results, then plot and compare the models and their versions using matplotlib and possibly seaborn. I finished the day working on my slides for tomorrow's presentation. It was a productive, interactive, and fun day. I learned a lot and can't wait to see the progress we've made once training finishes.
